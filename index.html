About Photosynthesis

The big picture, before you get into the raw materials.

🌿 What is photosynthesis?

Photosynthesis is the process plants (and some algae and bacteria) use to build their own food. Inside their leaves, tiny green structures called chloroplasts capture energy from sunlight and use it to turn simple raw materials into glucose — a sugar the plant burns for energy or stores for later.

It's the reason almost every food chain on Earth works at all: plants are the "producers" that everything else, directly or indirectly, ends up eating.

🧮 The word equation

Carbon Dioxide + Water --(Light + Chlorophyll)--> Glucose + Oxygen

In symbols: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O --(sunlight, chlorophyll)--> C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

The two stages

1. Light-dependent reactionsHappen in the thylakoid membranes; sunlight splits water and releases oxygen
2. Light-independent reactionsHappen in the stroma; CO₂ is built into glucose using energy from stage 1

🌍 Why it matters

🍎 FoodThe base of nearly every food chain on Earth
💨 OxygenThe main source of breathable oxygen in our atmosphere
🌡️ ClimateRemoves huge amounts of CO₂ from the air every year

This video walks through the full photosynthesis process step by step — how sunlight, water and carbon dioxide combine inside a leaf's chloroplasts to produce glucose and release oxygen. Watch it for a quick visual refresher before diving into the Raw Materials below.

Key idea: Photosynthesis uses light reactions to make ATP, powering the Calvin Cycle, which fixes CO₂ into glucose.

About Gravity

The big picture, before you get into the raw materials.

🪐 What is gravity?

Gravity is the invisible pulling force that exists between any two objects that have mass. It's what pulls a dropped apple to the ground, keeps your feet on the floor, and holds the Moon in orbit around the Earth.

Every object with mass pulls on every other object with mass — even you and this device are pulling on each other very slightly! The bigger the masses, and the closer they are, the stronger that pull becomes.

🧮 The formula

Force  =  G × (Mass 1 × Mass 2) ÷ Distance²

In symbols: F = G m₁m₂ ⁄ r²

Two key rules

1. More massBigger objects pull harder on everything around them
2. More distanceThe pull weakens fast — double the distance and the force drops to a quarter

🌍 Why it matters

🍎 Falling objectsThe reason anything you drop always falls down, not sideways
🛰️ OrbitsKeeps moons, planets and satellites circling instead of flying off into space
🌊 TidesThe Moon's pull on Earth's oceans is what creates high and low tide

🎥 Video

Watch it for a quick visual refresher before diving into the Raw Materials below.

Key idea: Gravity follows F = Gm₁m₂ ⁄ r², balancing mass, distance and force to explain orbital motion.

About Forces

The push and pull behind every bit of motion.

💪 What is a force?

A force is simply a push or a pull. It's what starts something moving, stops it, speeds it up, slows it down, or changes the direction it's travelling in — and it can even change an object's shape.

Some forces need contact, like pushing a door or kicking a ball. Others act from a distance without ever touching, like gravity, magnetism, or static electricity.

🧮 The formula

Force  =  Mass × Acceleration

In symbols: F = m × a

Newton's three laws

1. InertiaAn object stays still or keeps moving steadily unless a force acts on it
2. F = maThe force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration
3. Action–ReactionEvery force has an equal and opposite force pushing back

🌍 Why it matters

🚗 MotionEvery speed-up, stop, and turn you feel in a car or on a bike is a force at work
🏗️ StructuresEngineers balance forces so bridges and buildings stay standing
⚽ SportsKicking, throwing and jumping are all about how much force you apply

🎥 Video

This video breaks down what a force is, Newton's three laws in action, and how F = m × a ties motion, mass and acceleration together. Watch it for a quick visual refresher before diving into the Raw Materials below.

Key idea: Force equals mass times acceleration (F = ma) — heavier objects need more force to speed up or slow down at the same rate.

About Matter & Phase Transitions

The big picture, before you get into the raw materials.

🧊 What is matter?

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space — the water in a glass, the air you breathe, the chair you're sitting on. Matter usually shows up in one of three everyday states: solid, liquid, or gas.

Which state a substance is in depends on how much heat energy its particles have. Add or remove heat, and the same substance can change from one state to another — that's called a phase transition.

🧮 The state cycle

Solid  ⇌  Liquid  ⇌  Gas

Adding heat energy pushes matter from solid → liquid → gas; removing it reverses the journey.

Two special points

Melting / Freezing pointThe exact temperature where a solid and liquid switch places
Boiling / Condensation pointThe exact temperature where a liquid and gas switch places

🌍 Why it matters

🧊 Everyday lifeIce melting, water boiling, and steam rising are all phase changes in action
🏭 IndustryEngineers control phase changes to purify, cool, and manufacture materials
🌦️ WeatherThe water cycle runs entirely on evaporation, condensation and freezing

This video walks through matter and phase transitions step by step — how heat energy moves particles between solid, liquid and gas. Watch it for a quick visual refresher before diving into the Raw Materials below.

Key idea: Phase transitions show heat energy breaking molecular bonds — causing temperature to plateau at melting and boiling points instead of rising instantly.
