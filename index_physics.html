Gravity

The big picture, before you get into the gravity parameters.

🪐 What is gravity?

Gravity is the invisible pulling force that exists between any two objects that have mass. It's what pulls a dropped apple to the ground, keeps your feet on the floor, and holds the Moon in orbit around the Earth.

Every object with mass pulls on every other object with mass — even you and this device are pulling on each other very slightly! The bigger the masses, and the closer they are, the stronger that pull becomes.

🧮 The formula

Force  =  G × (Mass 1 × Mass 2) ÷ Distance²

In symbols: F = G m₁m₂ ⁄ r²

🎥 Video

This video breaks down how gravity works — why every object with mass pulls on every other object, how that pull grows stronger with more mass and weaker with more distance, and what that looks like in everyday life, from falling objects to orbits. Watch it for a quick visual refresher before diving into the Gravity Parameters below.

Key idea: Gravity follows F = Gm₁m₂ ⁄ r², balancing mass, distance and force to explain orbital motion.

🌊 Daily Life Applications

Gravity is at work all around you — here's where to spot it.

🛰️ Why do satellites stay in orbit?

A satellite is always falling toward Earth — but it's also moving sideways so fast that the ground curves away beneath it just as quickly as it falls. That constant "falling and missing" is what an orbit actually is.

🌊 Why do tides occur?

The Moon pulls harder on the side of Earth facing it than on the far side, stretching the oceans into two bulges. As Earth spins through those bulges, coastlines experience rising and falling tides roughly twice a day.

🧑‍🚀 Why do astronauts float?

Astronauts aren't in a gravity-free zone — at typical space-station altitude, Earth's gravity is still about 90% as strong as on the ground. They float because the station and everything inside it are continuously falling together, so there's nothing to push back against them.

ℹ️ About This App

EcoExplore is an interactive science learning platform that walks students through Biology, Physics and Chemistry topics with guided lessons, hands-on virtual experiments and auto-graded quizzes — while giving teachers and admins live visibility into every student's progress.

Every topic follows the same guided chain — Materials → Products → Experiments → Quiz — and each stage only unlocks once the one before it has actually been explored and submitted, so progress always reflects real engagement, not just clicking through.
